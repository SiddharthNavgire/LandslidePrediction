Slope Exposition (Aspect °)

Aspect (slope orientation) affects the exposure to sunlight, wind and precipitation thereby indirectly affecting other factors that contribute to landslides such as soil moisture, vegetation cover and soil thickness.

Distance to streams (m)

The distance to streams is a measurement that indicates how far a specific location is from the nearest stream or river. The proximity to streams can impact the stability of the surrounding terrain. Various factors associated with streams, such as water flow, erosion, and soil saturation, can contribute to an increased risk of landslides.

Catchment (basin area m2)

A catchment or basin refers to an area of land where water from rainfall or snowmelt drains downhill into a common outlet, such as a river, lake, or ocean. The features of a catchment or basin can influence landslide occurrence in several ways.

Slope curvature (+,-)

In the context of landslides, slope curvature refers to the change in slope direction over a specific distance. Positive slope curvature indicates a convex shape, where the terrain is curving outward, while negative slope curvature indicates a concave shape, where the terrain is curving inward.

Contour-parallel slope curvature

Contour-parallel slope curvature is a topographic feature that plays a significant role in understanding and predicting landslides. This feature refers to the curvature of the terrain along contour lines on a slope. It helps identify concave and convex shapes in the landscape.

Profile-parallel slope curvature

Profile-parallel slope curvature is a terrain feature that plays a role in landslide susceptibility. It refers to the curvature of the land surface along the slope, specifically parallel to the slope direction. This feature is significant in landslide studies because it provides insights into the shape and morphology of the terrain.

Change in height derived from slope(m)

Areas with significant changes in elevation along the slope may be more susceptible to landslides. Especially when combined with other relevant features like lithology Dependent on slope

Absolute change in height above valley floor or DROP(m)

Absolute change in height above valley floor. "Drop" measures the absolute change in height from a specific location to the elevation of the valley floor. It indicates how much the terrain drops vertically from a given point to the valley floor. Areas with larger drops might be more prone to landslides

Distance to rock(m)

"Rockdist" measures the distance from a target location to the nearest rock feature. It provides information about how far the point is from rock formations in the terrain. Smaller values of "rockdist" indicate that the point is closer to the nearest rock feature. Larger values suggest that the point is farther away from rock formations.

The Factor of Safety (FoS)

The Factor of Safety (FoS) is defined as the ratio of the forces resisting slope failure to the forces driving slope failure. It provides a safety margin and is used to assess whether a slope is stable or if there is a risk of failure.
FoS > 1 : Indicates that the slope is considered stable because the resisting forces are greater than the driving forces. A higher factor of safety implies a larger safety margin.
Fos < 1:Indicates that the slope is potentially unstable, and failure could occur.

Failure scarp and it's distance(m)

"scarp" refers to a steep slope or cliff formed by the vertical displacement of the Earth's surface along a fault or rupture zone.
0/1 : is the scarp present or not
Scarp distance represents the horizontal distance between the headscarp (upper edge) and the toescarp (bottom or toe) of a landslide, and it is measured in linear units.
lower the value of scarp distance, lower the chances of landslide.
higher the value of scarp distance, higher the chances of landslide.

Friction Angle(°)

The friction angle, often denoted as "ϕ" (phi), is an angle measured between the horizontal plane and the inclined plane along which soil or rock mass begins to slide or fail.

Slope gradient(°) and Slope length(m)

The slope gradient is a measure of the steepness of the land surface and is defined as the ratio of the vertical change in elevation to the horizontal distance. Steep slopes with a high gradient are more prone to landslides. The gravitational force acting on the soil and rock materials increases with the slope steepness. High slope gradients can lead to reduced stability, making the slope susceptible to various types of landslides, such as rockfalls, debris flows, or shallow slides.

Vegetation

Vegetation acts as a natural shield, intercepting rainfall and mitigating its direct impact on the soil surface. This protective land cover significantly reduces the risk of soil saturation and the potential triggers for landslides.

Specific Weight(kN/m3)

specific weight is the force exerted by gravity on a unit volume of a substance. formula = weight(force)/volume
